## Why This Exists

AI-assisted creation occupies an unsettled legal landscape. Copyright law was
written for human authors, and courts, legislatures, and copyright offices are
still determining how — or whether — it extends to AI-generated output. ORGANVM
produces code, documentation, creative works, and discourse using AI assistance
across all eight organs. The copyright status of that output directly affects
licensing, attribution, and commercial viability.

This document tracks key rulings, regulatory guidance, and publisher policies to
ensure ORGANVM's practices remain defensible as the law evolves.

**Disclaimer**: This is a reference tracking document, not legal advice. Consult
qualified legal counsel for specific copyright questions.

## Case Law and Regulatory Guidance

| Case/Ruling | Date | Jurisdiction | Key Holding | Impact on ORGANVM |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **USCO Registration Guidance** (89 FR 16190) | Feb 2023, updated Mar 2024 | US (Federal) | AI-generated content is not copyrightable; human-authored elements in AI-assisted works may be registered if human made creative choices | All purely AI-generated output must be treated as public domain; human selection, arrangement, and editing create copyrightable elements |
| **Thaler v. Perlmutter** | Aug 2023 | US (D.D.C.) | AI cannot be named as author or inventor; copyright requires human authorship | Confirms ORGANVM must attribute human as author; AI is a tool, not a creator |
| **Zarya of the Dawn** (USCO) | Feb 2023 | US (USCO) | Partial copyright granted: human-selected arrangement of AI images copyrightable; individual AI-generated images not copyrightable | Supports ORGANVM's model — human directs (copyrightable), AI generates (not independently copyrightable), human reviews and arranges (copyrightable) |
| **OpenAI court order** (discovery rulings) | 2023–2024 | US (various) | Courts ordering disclosure of training data sources and practices | Training data transparency becoming a judicial expectation; ORGANVM should document what models are used and for what |
| **NYT v. OpenAI** | Filed Dec 2023, pending | US (S.D.N.Y.) | Alleges AI training on copyrighted news articles constitutes infringement; fair use defense contested | If NYT prevails, could restrict AI model availability or increase costs; if OpenAI prevails, confirms broad fair use for training. ORGANVM's risk is as a user, not a trainer |
| **Andersen v. Stability AI** | Filed Jan 2023, pending | US (N.D. Cal.) | Artists allege AI image generators trained on copyrighted art without permission | Similar implications to NYT — affects model availability, not ORGANVM's direct practices |
| **Getty Images v. Stability AI** | Filed Feb 2023, pending | US (D. Del.) / UK | Alleges unauthorized use of Getty's image library for training | Reinforces training data provenance concerns; ORGANVM uses models, does not train them |

## ORGANVM Implications and Practices

### Principle: Human Direction Creates Copyrightability

Based on current law (Zarya of the Dawn, USCO guidance), ORGANVM's AI-conductor
model is well-positioned:

1. **Human selects** the task, scope, and constraints → creative choice
2. **AI generates** candidate output → not independently copyrightable
3. **Human reviews, edits, and arranges** → creative expression
4. **Human approves** for integration → final creative decision

This four-step process maps directly to the USCO's framework for evaluating
human authorship in AI-assisted works.

### Practice Requirements

#### For Code (Organs I, II, III, IV)

- **Copyrightable**: Human-directed code where the developer specifies architecture,
  selects from AI suggestions, modifies output, and integrates into a larger system
- **Not copyrightable**: Verbatim AI output accepted without modification
- **Recommendation**: Always modify, review, and integrate AI-generated code rather
  than accepting wholesale. The Frame/Shape/Build/Prove workflow naturally enforces this

#### For Documentation (All Organs)

- **Copyrightable**: Human-structured documents where AI assists with drafting but
  human determines structure, selects content, and edits for accuracy
- **Not copyrightable**: AI-generated boilerplate accepted without editing
- **Recommendation**: Use AI for drafting assistance; always review and restructure

#### For Creative Works (Organ II — Poiesis)

- **Highest risk area**: Generative art, music, performance systems
- **Copyrightable**: Works where human makes substantive creative choices — selecting
  parameters, curating outputs, arranging elements, post-processing
- **Not copyrightable**: Pure AI generation with minimal human involvement
- **Recommendation**: Document human creative decisions in AI interaction manifests (F-43);
  maintain evidence of human direction for each creative work

#### For Discourse (Organ V — Logos)

- **Publisher compliance**: All published essays, articles, and analyses must include
  AI usage disclosure per IEEE/ACM/Nature standards
- **Recommendation**: Include standardized AI disclosure footer in all Logos publications

### Evidence Preservation

To defend copyright claims on AI-assisted works, ORGANVM should maintain:

1. **AI interaction manifests** (F-43) — record of prompts, model used, human
   direction given, modifications made
2. **Git history** — commit-level evidence of human edits to AI-generated content
3. **Session transcripts** — available on-demand via `organvm session transcript`
4. **Prompt templates** — documented in `prompt-template-library.md`, showing human
   creative framework applied to AI generation

These records serve as evidence of human authorship should copyright be challenged.

## Risk Assessment

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI output deemed uncopyrightable | High (established) | Medium — affects licensing of pure AI output | Always apply human editing and arrangement |
| Training data lawsuits restrict model access | Medium (pending cases) | Medium — may increase costs or limit providers | Maintain multi-provider capability (F-40 gateway) |
| New legislation requires AI disclosure | High (trend) | Low — ORGANVM already documents AI usage | Maintain AI interaction manifests (F-43) |
| Publisher rejects AI-assisted submissions | Medium | Low — can rewrite without AI assistance (F-70) | Maintain AI-free practice cadence |
| Retroactive copyright invalidation | Low | High — would affect existing published works | Document human contribution comprehensively |
